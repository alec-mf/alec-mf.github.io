Ketuta Alexi-Meskhishvili (1979) is a Georgian photographer based in Berlin, Germany. She grew up in New York, where she studied the practice at Bard College. Alexi-Meskhishvili’s work converges the boundaries between analog and digital photography. Her recent solo exhibitions include ‘Hollow Body’ at the Andrea Rosen Gallery, New York, and Kölnischer Kunstverein, Cologne.

Kai Althoff (1966) is a German multimedia contemporary artist living and working in Cologne. His work often explores German folklore and everyday life, and elements of German expressionism. Althoff’s art has been exhibited in The Museum of Modern Art in New York and the ICA Boston.

Leilah Babirye (1985) is a Ugandan multidisciplinary artist based in New York City. After being outed for her Queer identity in Uganda, the artist sought and was granted asylum. Her abstract sculptures put topics of sexuality and human rights into conversation. Babirye had solo exhibits at Rebecca Camacho Presents in San Francisco and Gordon Robichaux in New York. The Trapholt Museum, Kolding, Denmark, and the FIAR: Fire Island Residency Exhibition in Long Island, NY also featured her work in group exhibitions.

I am an artist-activist recently granted asylum in the United States from discrimination upheld by the 2009 Anti-Homosexuality Bill in my native Uganda. After participating in the Fire Island Artist Residency in 2015, I decided to remain in New York as my life was at risk if I returned home. I left behind: my artwork, my studio, my girlfriend, and my family. The one thing I have not stopped doing is making art; I use my work to advocate for queer rights and the voiceless.

Combining figurative and abstract elements, and employing a wide range of found objects, my sculptures portray queer archetypes that I’ve named after contemporary princes, princesses, and clans within Buganda’s royal family. Through the act of burning, nailing, and assembling I address the realities of being gay in the context of Uganda, Africa, and the world at large, conflating identities and challenging traditional ideas of gender and power.

Recently my working process has been fueled by a need to locate myself within my new environment, incorporating new materials and subjects into my vocabulary. Portraits of my transgender friends rendered on paper, ceramic, and carved wood portray some of the most vulnerable members of our community.
